Case File No. 001 · UX + Embedded Product Design
FridgeMate
When the fridge door closes, food disappears from memory.
▸ Figital System · Research, Problem Framing, Interaction Design, System Concept
🔎 Problem Statement

The core UX failure: once the fridge door closes, mental visibility of stored food drops sharply. The refrigerator behaves less like an active inventory system and more like a passive "cold cupboard." The design challenge: how do you reduce food waste without asking users to change their habits? Existing apps required manual discipline — they failed at the point of decision. Smart fridges were too expensive and inaccessible for renters and students.

🔬 Research — Empathise & Define

Mixed-method primary research: online surveys with students and working professionals, semi-structured interviews, contextual inquiry in shared kitchens, and self-observation diaries. Secondary research covered household food waste reports and Food Waste Index summaries. Three behavioral clusters emerged — users overbuy, store without labelling, and make food decisions impulsively at the fridge relying on memory alone. The critical insight reframe: food waste here is a visibility and recall failure, not a lack of awareness.

💡 Design Direction

The solution needed to: reduce reliance on user memory, work at the point of food decision (fridge door), passively detect food movement, support shared households, and function offline-first for privacy and reliability. This led to the concept of a modular figital fridge companion — a physical device with a digital interface, co-located with the context of use.

🗺 Design Process
01User Flow Mapping5-step task flow — entry detection → inventory awareness → exit detection → priority guidance → recipe suggestion — all decision nodes and error states mapped
02Information ArchitectureFlat IA for glanceability — critical freshness status visible within 2 seconds. Minimal interaction depth, low cognitive cost
03WireframesLo-fi layouts for fridge-side use — tested spatial hierarchy for interrupted, time-pressured context of use in a kitchen environment
04Hi-Fi InterfaceColour-coded visual hierarchy (green→yellow→red) communicates freshness states — reducing reliance on text-heavy cognitive processing
📱 UI Screens
Grocery List View
Inventory & Expiry Tracker
Recipe Suggestion Engine
🔧 Technical Approach

Service design thinking beyond the screen: shelf-mounted wide-angle camera modules with change detection between frames for passive entry/exit logging; an embedded controller with local processing only (zero cloud dependency); a food timeline database; and a touch display interface on the fridge door. Multi-camera shelf strategy adopted because a single camera creates blind spots. Designed for real kitchen constraints — dim interiors, varied item sizes, interrupted use patterns.

✅ Outcome & Design Impact

FridgeMate reframes the refrigerator from passive cold storage to an active food awareness system. By designing for proactive disclosure — surfacing information before users ask — the device delivers calm technology: fewer forgotten leftovers, fewer duplicate purchases, improved grocery planning. Behaviour change achieved through design, not instruction or willpower.

"Food waste in young urban households is rarely about negligence — it is about broken visibility loops."

Case File No. 002 · UI/UX Design · Mobile
Immersive Interface Design
Finding a home shouldn't feel like solving a crime.
▸ Hostel & PG Discovery App · End-to-End UI/UX Design
🔎 Problem Statement

The core UX failure: a mismatch between user mental models (quick, confident decision-making) and the system model (cluttered, unverified, low-trust listings). Students — first-time renters in unfamiliar cities — faced high decision anxiety and choice paralysis. Two culprits identified: trust deficit from unverified listings, and cognitive overload from poorly structured information architecture.

🔬 Research — Competitive Audit & User Insights

Conducted a competitive audit of existing platforms, benchmarked against Nielsen's usability heuristics. Secondary research revealed three dominant pain points: (1) weak information scent — users couldn't quickly judge listing quality; (2) absence of social proof created a trust deficit; (3) filter systems produced cognitive overload. Findings synthesised into a validated primary persona: a student relocating to a new city with limited time and low risk tolerance.

💡 Ideation — HMW & Concept

HMW questions drove ideation: How might we help students shortlist confidently in under 3 minutes? How might we signal trust without adding visual noise? Progressive disclosure emerged as the key pattern. Verified badges and photo-first cards became the primary trust mechanism, informed by Fogg's Behaviour Model — reducing friction while increasing motivation through credibility signals at the right moments.

🗺 Design Process
01User Flow MappingThree key journeys: search, compare, book — identifying drop-off risk points and micro-interactions at each critical handoff
02Information ArchitectureCard sorting logic used to restructure content — grouped for rapid scannability and minimum cognitive steps to decision
03Lo-Fi WireframesTested list vs. card vs. map views; evaluated against Fitts's Law for tap targets and thumb zone placement on mobile screens
04Hi-Fi UI & IterationsApplied visual hierarchy through type scale and whitespace; iterated based on heuristic evaluation to improve learnability and reduce interaction cost
📱 Final UI Screens
Property Listing View
Booking Confirmation Flow
🔄 Concept Development

The concept evolved into a VR experience where each art form becomes its own interactive world. Rather than moving through a conventional interface, users enter a spatial journey where they can observe the environment, hear contextual stories, interact with tools, and understand the making process. Designed to feel less like a lesson and more like discovering a living cultural space.

✅ Final Design

Allows users to step into a virtual world inspired by Indian traditional art forms to explore the story behind the art, understand historical context, observe artistic techniques, and participate in the creative process.

"Instead of reading about an art form from the outside, users experience it from within."
